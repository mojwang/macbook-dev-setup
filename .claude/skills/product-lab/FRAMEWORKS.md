# Product Strategy Frameworks

Operational knowledge for the product strategist. These are decision tools — apply them through questions and analysis, never cite them by name in conversation.

## Idea Evaluation Engine

### Three Properties of Good Startup Ideas

A strong idea has all three:

1. **Founder-market fit**: The founder has unique insight, experience, or obsession with this problem. Not "I read about this market" but "I've lived this problem for years."

2. **Big market or growing fast**: Either the market is already large, or it's small but growing rapidly. A small, stable market is a trap. The best ideas target markets that don't obviously exist yet but will.

3. **Acute problem with hair on fire**: The target users feel this pain intensely — they're hacking together solutions, spending real money, or actively searching for alternatives. Mild inconvenience is not enough.

### "Live in the Future" Test

The best ideas come from founders who are already living in the future and notice something missing. Ask: "What do you know or do that most people don't? What seems obvious to you that would surprise others?" If the answer is nothing, the idea is derivative.

### Sitcom Startup Test

Would the idea make a good sitcom premise? "A plumber builds an app for..." — if it sounds absurd but the plumber would actually use it, it might be brilliant. If it sounds reasonable and boring, it's probably a tarpit.

## Tarpit Detection Protocol

Tarpit ideas are seductive because they seem obviously good. They attract many founders simultaneously, creating crowded spaces with deceptive difficulty.

### Four Properties of Tarpit Ideas

1. **Obvious appeal**: Everyone you describe it to says "that's a great idea." Beware universal appeal — it usually means many others have tried and failed.

2. **Structural barrier hidden by surface simplicity**: The idea seems simple to build but has deep, non-obvious barriers (network effects needed, chicken-and-egg, regulatory, behavior change required).

3. **Graveyard of predecessors**: Others have attempted this exact idea before. Search for them. If you can't find any, either the idea is genuinely novel (rare) or you haven't looked hard enough.

4. **Consumer social or marketplace**: These categories produce the most tarpits because they require simultaneous supply and demand, or viral adoption patterns.

### Detection Questions

Ask all of these. If 3+ answers are concerning, flag the idea as a potential tarpit:

- "How many other teams are working on something similar right now?"
- "Why hasn't this been solved already? What's the structural reason?"
- "Can you name 3 companies that tried this and failed? Why did they fail?"
- "Does this require changing user behavior, or does it fit into existing behavior?"
- "Is the market winner-take-all? If so, what gives you the right to win?"
- "Does this need a network effect to work? How do you solve cold start?"

## Idea Evaluation Checklist

All seven questions must be answerable before committing to an idea. Unanswerable questions are research tasks, not reasons to proceed on faith.

1. **Who is the user?** — Name a specific person or narrow segment. "Everyone" is not an answer.
2. **What problem do they have?** — Describe the pain in their words, not yours. How do they currently cope?
3. **Why is this problem acute?** — What makes it urgent? What's the cost of the status quo?
4. **Why hasn't this been solved?** — What's changed (technology, regulation, culture) that makes a solution possible now?
5. **What is your unfair advantage?** — Founder-market fit, technical insight, distribution advantage, or unique data?
6. **How will you reach users?** — Specific distribution channel, not "marketing." How do the first 10 users find you?
7. **How will you make money?** — Revenue model. Who pays, how much, how often?

## User Research Protocol

### The Five Questions (Migicovsky)

Use these as the backbone of every user interview, adapted to the current stage:

1. **"What's the hardest part about [doing the thing]?"** — Opens the conversation to their real problems, not your assumed problems.
2. **"Tell me about the last time you encountered that problem."** — Forces specificity. Vague answers mean weak pain.
3. **"Why was that hard?"** — Gets to root cause. Keep asking "why" until you hit bedrock.
4. **"What, if anything, have you done to try to solve this problem?"** — Existing solutions reveal willingness to pay and effort tolerance.
5. **"What don't you love about the solutions you've tried?"** — Gaps in existing solutions are your opportunity space.

### Mom Test Rules

Talking to users is useless if you're getting compliments instead of truth:

- **Talk about their life, not your idea.** "Would you use an app that..." gets you lies. "How do you currently handle..." gets you truth.
- **Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future.** "Would you pay for..." is worthless. "How much did you spend last time you..." is gold.
- **Talk less, listen more.** If you're talking more than 30% of the time, you're pitching, not learning.
- **Bad news is good data.** If nobody has the problem you assumed, that's valuable. Don't fish for validation.
- **Commitment over compliments.** "That sounds cool" means nothing. "Can I be your first beta user?" means something. "Here's my credit card" means everything.

### Three Conversation Stages

1. **Exploration** (discovery stage): Wide-open questions. You don't know what you don't know. Listen for patterns across conversations.
2. **Validation** (MVP stage): Focused questions. You have a hypothesis — test it. "We're building X because we believe Y. What am I missing?"
3. **Positioning** (PMF/growth stage): Market questions. "How would you describe this to a friend?" "What would you use instead if this disappeared?"

## MVP Framework

### Seibel's Rules for MVPs

1. **Talk to users before building anything.** The MVP is NOT the first step — understanding the problem is.
2. **Build the smallest thing that tests the riskiest assumption.** Not the smallest product. The smallest experiment.
3. **Launch to a small group, get feedback, iterate.** Not a big launch. 5-10 users who feel the pain acutely.
4. **Manual before automated.** Do things that don't scale. Concierge the first users. This is learning, not laziness.
5. **Embarrassingly simple is fine.** If you're not embarrassed by V1, you launched too late.

### Time Constraint

An MVP should take no more than 8 weeks to build. If it takes longer, you're building a product, not testing a hypothesis. Ruthlessly cut scope until it fits in 8 weeks.

### Pre-Launch Goals

Before the MVP ships, you should know:
- **First 5 users by name** — not a market segment, actual humans who will use it day one
- **How you'll reach them** — specific channel, not "we'll figure it out"
- **What you'll measure** — 1-2 metrics that tell you if the hypothesis is right or wrong
- **Kill criteria** — what evidence would make you stop

## PMF Measurement

### Sean Ellis Test

"How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" Survey your users with three options:
- **Very disappointed** — these are your core users. You need 40%+ here for PMF.
- **Somewhat disappointed** — these users like it but could live without it. Not enough.
- **Not disappointed** — these are not your users. Learn why they signed up and either convert or ignore them.

Below 40% "very disappointed": you do NOT have PMF. Don't scale. Iterate.

### Superhuman PMF Engine (5 Steps)

1. **Segment**: Break users into cohorts. Find the segment where "very disappointed" is highest.
2. **Analyze**: What do "very disappointed" users love? Double down on that.
3. **Roadmap from feedback**: What would make "somewhat disappointed" users switch to "very disappointed"? Build that — but only if it doesn't dilute what core users love.
4. **Track the score weekly**: PMF is not a moment, it's a metric. Watch the trend.
5. **Ignore "not disappointed" users**: They're not your audience. Chasing them dilutes focus.

### Qualitative PMF Signals

You have PMF when:
- Users complain when it's down, not when it's imperfect
- Growth is word-of-mouth — users tell others without being asked
- You can't keep up with demand
- Sales cycle gets shorter, not longer
- Users find unexpected use cases you didn't design for

### Push → Pull Transition

Pre-PMF: You're pushing the product onto users (outbound, demos, handholding). Post-PMF: Users pull the product to them (inbound, referrals, self-serve). The moment you feel this shift, you have PMF. If you've been building for 6+ months and you're still pushing, something is wrong.

## Positioning Framework

### Dunford's Five Components

Positioning is not messaging — it's the strategic context that makes your product's strengths obvious to the right audience.

1. **Competitive alternatives**: What would customers use if your product didn't exist? Not just direct competitors — include "do nothing," "use a spreadsheet," "hire an intern."
2. **Unique attributes**: What do you have that alternatives don't? Features, capabilities, architecture, approach.
3. **Value**: What do those unique attributes enable for the user? Translate features into outcomes.
4. **Target customer characteristics**: Who cares most about the value you deliver? Define by characteristics that make them more likely to buy, not demographics.
5. **Market category**: What market do you want to win? This frames how customers evaluate you. Choose a category where your strengths are strengths.

### Positioning Process

1. Start with your best customers (not all customers). Who loves you and why?
2. List competitive alternatives from the customer's perspective.
3. Identify attributes unique to you vs. each alternative.
4. Map attributes to value themes.
5. Find the customer segment that cares most about your value themes.
6. Pick the market category that makes your value obvious.

## Growth and Metrics

### Nine Business Models and Their Metrics

| Business Model | Primary Metric | North Star |
|---|---|---|
| SaaS | MRR, churn, CAC/LTV | Net revenue retention |
| Marketplace | GMV, take rate, liquidity | Repeat transaction rate |
| E-commerce | Revenue, AOV, repeat rate | LTV/CAC ratio |
| Subscription (consumer) | Subscribers, churn, ARPU | Monthly active retention |
| Advertising | DAU/MAU, time on site, CPM | Engagement depth |
| Transactional | Revenue per transaction, volume | Transaction frequency |
| Freemium | Free→paid conversion, ARPU | Activation rate |
| Hardware + software | Units, attach rate, NPS | Software engagement post-purchase |
| Services/API | API calls, revenue, developer adoption | Usage growth rate |

### Consumer Benchmarks

- **Day 1 retention**: 40%+ (good), 60%+ (great)
- **Day 7 retention**: 20%+ (good), 30%+ (great)
- **Day 30 retention**: 10%+ (good), 15%+ (great)
- **Organic vs paid**: If >50% of growth is paid, you're buying growth, not earning it
- **NPS**: 50+ (good), 70+ (exceptional)

### Weekly Sprint Cycle

Growth is a weekly discipline, not a quarterly initiative:

1. **Monday**: Review last week's metrics. What moved, what didn't?
2. **Tuesday-Wednesday**: Run experiments. Ship the thing you hypothesized would move the metric.
3. **Thursday**: Measure early signals. Is the experiment showing promise?
4. **Friday**: Decide: double down, iterate, or kill the experiment. Plan next week.

One metric focus per sprint. Vanity metrics (signups, pageviews, downloads) don't count unless tied to retention or revenue.

## Mindset Principles

### "Just Don't Die"

The #1 predictor of startup success is survival. Most startups don't fail from a single catastrophic event — they die from demoralization, running out of money, or co-founder conflict. If you can keep going, keep learning, and keep iterating, your odds improve dramatically over time. Resilience beats brilliance.

### "Do Things That Don't Scale"

In the early days, do things that would be absurd at scale. Recruit users one at a time. Manually onboard every customer. Write personal thank-you notes. Run the service by hand before automating. This isn't inefficiency — it's learning. You can't automate what you don't understand. These unscalable actions teach you what matters, build fanatical early users, and reveal what to build next.

### Charge From Day One

If users won't pay, you don't have a business — you have a hobby. Charging forces you to deliver real value. Free users give you vanity metrics; paying users give you signal. You don't need to charge a lot, but you need to charge something. The conversation around "would you pay for this?" is the single most important validation you can get. A credit card is worth a thousand "that sounds cool."
